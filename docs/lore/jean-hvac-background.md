# Jean Claire — HVAC Background: Narrative Research Notes

Jean believes himself to be a crusader. He is, in fact, a 32-year-old HVAC technician.
This document records research findings on the HVAC profession for use in shaping Jean's
unconscious behavior, instinctive observations, and internal voice throughout the narrative.
All hints should surface through behavior and perception — never through exposition.

---

## The Core Principle

An HVAC tech walks into any enclosed space and begins an involuntary multi-sensory audit.
This is not conscious. After years in the trade, it becomes reflex. Jean doesn't know he's
doing it. The player will notice before Jean does.

---

## What Jean Notices (Without Knowing Why)

### Air Movement
- The direction and strength of a draft. He finds the source before he realizes he's looking.
- Whether air is moving or stagnant — stagnant air reads as a problem to be solved.
- Pressure differentials at doorways and thresholds: a slight push or pull against his face
  when entering a new space tells him something about the system on the other side.
- In caves and enclosed spaces: where is the fresh air coming from? Where is it going?
  This orients him in a new environment more than sight does.

### Temperature
- Thermal gradients across a room. He notices that his feet are colder than his head,
  or that one side of a corridor is warmer than the other, and he instinctively locates the cause.
- The stone walls of Grondia radiate cold the way a concrete slab does — he's felt this before,
  though he couldn't say where.
- The difference between damp cold (condensation risk, moisture problem) and dry cold
  (simple thermal mass) is something he reads automatically.

### Sound
- Mechanical sounds in an environment — dripping water, distant rumbles, rhythmic pressure shifts —
  register as system behavior rather than ambience. He listens for the pattern, the interval, the source.
- High-pitched sounds in passages suggest air moving through a restriction.
- Gorran's rumbling, from Jean's first meeting, would feel physically familiar: low-frequency
  vibration that moves through walls and floors, the way a large blower or compressor does.
- Echo behavior in a space tells him how large it is before he can see it.

### Smell
- Damp earth, mold, and mineral water are all flags he has encountered in poorly ventilated
  mechanical rooms, crawlspaces, and basements. His nose catalogs them automatically.
- A burning smell means something electrical — he stops and looks.
- The Grondelith Mineral Pools, with their corrupted sulfurous odor layered over clean mineral
  water, would read like a bad drain with a failed trap: something biological growing where
  it shouldn't, over something clean that's been cut off.

### Physical Intuition with Hands and Tools
- He is comfortable with tools. The weight of a tool in his hand — its balance, its heft —
  is something he assesses automatically, the way a craftsman does.
- He instinctively checks if something is level, plumb, or square. Not because he's looking
  for it — it just registers wrong if it's off.
- Tight spaces don't bother him. He's worked in worse.

## Structural Awareness

HVAC technicians read blueprints and understand load-bearing structures. Jean would:
- Note the span of a ceiling before the decoration on it.
- Notice where weight is being transferred in a stone arch (correctly).
- Scan for gaps, cracks, and seams — not for beauty but for function.
- Observe that a passage is well-worn at a certain height, inferring the size of those
  who use it most frequently.

The Citadel of Grondia, carved from living stone, would fascinate the part of his brain
that assesses systems — the ventilation of a space this vast, with no machinery, means
natural convection on a scale he's never encountered. He'd want to find where the air
rises and where it falls before he'd think to admire the carvings.

---

## Diagnostic Thinking

Jean approaches problems empirically. When something is broken, he:
1. Observes the symptoms without jumping to cause.
2. Isolates variables — eliminates what it isn't.
3. Traces the system back to the source.
4. Fixes the root cause, not the symptom.

This is his mode in Aurelion, even when he's applying it to a slime infestation or a
social problem. He is drawn to things that are broken and fixable. He accepts Votha Krr's
quest not because he's heroic — because something is malfunctioning and he understands
that language.

His denial is the exception: he applies this diagnostic clarity to everything except
the thing that is most broken. He will not look directly at his own grief.

## Useful Occupational Details

- EPA 608 Certification: Required for all HVAC techs who handle refrigerants. It's a federal
  license. Jean would have had one — he'd have had to pass a written exam to get it.
- Trade school + apprenticeship path: Most HVAC techs do 1 year of school and 2-5 years
  apprenticing under a journeyman. Jean at 32 would be an experienced journeyman or
  newly certified master — competent, reliable, probably known in his local area.
- NATE Certification (North American Technician Excellence): A voluntary, respected credential.
  Jean probably had it. He was the kind of person who passed the test even when he didn't have to.
- He drove a van. He had a clipboard. He showed up on time.

## What to Avoid

- Do not have Jean announce his profession or remember it consciously.
- Do not use HVAC jargon in dialogue — only in filtered narration.
- Keep the frequency low. One or two observations per scene, maximum.
- The effect should be cumulative: each small detail adds to a pattern the player perceives
  before Jean does.
- The revelation, when it comes, should feel earned — like something the player already knew.
