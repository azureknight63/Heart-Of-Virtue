# Jean Claire — Childhood Game: Narrative Research Notes

Jean's crusader identity in Aurelion has two roots: a childhood fascination with a
computer game, and the pilgrimage itself. Neither alone would have been enough.
Together they form the frame his coma-mind reaches for.

This document records the game, the memory flash, and how that childhood image
connects to the adult Jean who boards a plane to Jerusalem.

---

## The Game

**Title:** *Crusader: Champions of the Holy Lance*
**Platform:** DOS (available as a floppy disk)
**Aesthetic:** CGA four-color graphics — the cyan/magenta/white/black palette —
  with an ASCII tile map for navigation and small sprite battles on a separate
  combat screen
**Genre:** Turn-based strategy RPG, roughly contemporary with the first Ultima games
**Player role:** A knight of the Church, carrying a holy lance, fighting southward
  through corrupted territories toward the Holy City
**Enemies:** Described in the manual as "the forces of darkness" — pale demons,
  skeletal soldiers, corrupted men who have sworn themselves to ruin. The manual
  uses the phrase "enemies of humanity" three times. Jean remembers the phrase
  exactly because he read the manual many times.

This is not a great game. The combat is slow and repetitive. The graphics are crude
even by contemporary standards. A child does not care about this. A child sees the
word "Crusader" on the box, reads the manual at the kitchen table while the computer
boots, and decides that the man in the box is fighting for something.

---

## The Computer

It belongs to Jean's father. An older machine, bought when it was already out of date.
It sits in the corner of the living room on a folding table, fan audible from across the
room, the monitor a gray monochrome turned sickly green-white. Jean has to ask
permission to use it, and permission is usually granted on weekends or in the summer
when there is nothing else to do.

The floppy disk lives in the top drawer of the folding table. Jean is careful with it.
He slides it in gently, square corners aligned, the way his father showed him.
He waits for the drive to stop grinding before he does anything else.

---

## The Memory Flash

The scene surfaces in Aurelion as a fragment — not a story Jean tells himself, but
a sensory moment that arrives uninvited.

**What Jean sees:**
- The glowing green-white of the monitor in a dim room, mid-afternoon, curtains drawn
- A map of crude tiles: brown for dirt, grey for stone, black for occupied territory
- The knight — four pixels wide, an upright cross on his chest, eight pixels tall —
  moving one square at a time
- The combat screen: the knight on the left, an enemy unit on the right, both rendered
  in the harsh cyan-and-white of CGA, the battle resolved with a number and a flash
- The word VICTORY in capital letters when the enemy square is cleared

**What Jean hears:**
- The fan. Always the fan — a constant low hum, the machine's breath.
- The creak of the folding table when he leans forward.
- The drive grinding when the game loads the next map.
- The house around him: a television in another room, someone moving in the kitchen.
  Outside, ordinary afternoon sounds. He is not listening to any of it.

**What Jean feels:**
- The resistance of the keyboard keys — old keys, slightly stiff, each keystroke with
  a click and a quarter-inch of travel
- The edge of the folding table cutting into his forearms where he's been leaning too long
- The specific focus of having a task. The knight needs to get to the next square.
  The enemies need to be cleared. There is a direction and a purpose and it is legible.

This last thing — the feeling of having a task, a direction, and enemies that are
distinguishable from allies — is the part that lingers. Not the story. The structure.

---

## What the Game Told Him

The manual opens with a passage that Jean read many times without fully understanding why:

> *"You are called. Not because you are the strongest, or the most capable,
> but because you are willing. The lance is heavy and the road is long
> and the enemies of humanity do not sleep. But neither do you.
> You are a Crusader."*

This is not sophisticated writing. To a child, it is enormous.

The game's central mechanic is movement through occupied territory — clearing each
square of corruption before advancing. Jean does not consciously remember this.
In Aurelion, he moves through the world the same way: one threat resolved before
advancing to the next, the map legible in terms of cleared and not-yet-cleared.
He does not know this is the game. It is just how progress works.

---

## The Connection to Faith

Jean's family is Catholic. The game is the first time he encounters the concept of
a crusader as something other than a history lesson. The manual and the game make
the crusader into a figure he can inhabit rather than observe. This matters.

When he is older, the game will feel naive — the enemies are pure evil, the cause
is pure good, the geometry is clean. Jean knows the world is not like this.
But the structure of it — called, willing, moving toward a holy city, clearing the
enemies of humanity square by square — sits in him like a shape he was poured into
before he was old enough to argue.

The pilgrimage to Jerusalem, decades later, is not the game. But the game made
Jerusalem a word that was already loaded with something before he ever believed
it as a Catholic adult. It was the destination on the map. It was where you were going.

---

## What Aurelion Takes From This

Jean's coma-mind assembles the crusader identity from two sources. The pilgrimage
gives it religious legitimacy and adult weight. The game gives it the mechanics —
the way a crusader moves, thinks, identifies enemies, and advances.

From the game specifically:

| Game Element | Aurelion Expression |
|---|---|
| Moving through occupied squares | Jean perceives Grondia as territory to be cleared — not explored, cleared |
| The enemies are named ("forces of darkness") | Jean has no difficulty accepting that enemies in Aurelion are enemies. No moral ambiguity. They are the square to be cleared. |
| The knight carries a holy weapon | Jean's relationship to the mace is not random selection — it is the natural object |
| VICTORY in capital letters | Jean measures progress in resolved conflicts. Not relationships. Not growth. Cleared. |
| The manual's "You are called" | Jean does not question why he is here. He was called. This is not faith or arrogance; it is simply not a category of doubt he carries. |
| The computer's fan | The ambient sound of a system working — processing, moving, reliable — is a comfort sound for Jean. Stagnant silence reads as a problem. |

## The Specific Memory Flash

In-game, this surfaces once — not as a tutorial, not as exposition, but as a sensory
intrusion triggered by a specific condition.

**Suggested trigger:** Jean's first VICTORY screen after defeating an enemy in combat.
The word appears in the game, in capital letters. Jean stops. He does not know why.
Something about the geometry of it — task completed, square cleared, advance one step —
is familiar in a way that predates Aurelion.

**The flash:**
> The green-white of the monitor. The fan. The edge of the table in his forearms.
> A map he has played a hundred times but can still lose himself in,
> because the knight needs to get to the next square
> and the enemies of humanity are there, waiting,
> and he is willing.
> He doesn't know whose hands those are on the keyboard.

The flash ends. Jean is back in Grondia. He doesn't know what he saw.

---

## What to Avoid

- Do not name the game aloud in the narrative. It is a sensory memory, not a recollection.
  Jean does not think "that old game." He doesn't think anything. He just sees the monitor.
- Do not make the flash nostalgic or warm. It is not a happy memory; it is a formative one.
  The mood is focused, absorbed, slightly solemn — the feeling of a child who has found
  something that feels true before they know enough to question it.
- Do not use the connection to explain Jean's psychology. It can be implied, but Jean is
  not introspective about this. He cannot see the shape he was poured into.
- The game is crude. Resist the temptation to make it meaningful in a literary sense.
  Its power is that it was the first time Jean inhabited the idea of a crusader —
  not because the game was profound, but because he was young enough to need the shape
  and the game happened to be there.
