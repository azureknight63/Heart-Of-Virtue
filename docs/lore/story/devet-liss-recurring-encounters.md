# Devet and Liss — Recurring Encounter Arc

## Overview

Devet and Liss are recurring minor characters — members of Mara's loose traveling
group who reappear at intervals across Jean's journey. As nomads moving their own
routes through the same world, they arrive naturally at some of the same places
Jean does: waypoints, crossings, the edges of territories that everyone passes
through sooner or later.

Their function in the narrative is to provide **respite and mirth** — a campfire
that doesn't ask anything of Jean, a meal that is simply a meal, and the specific
levity that comes from Liss's unfiltered curiosity and Devet's one-word deflations.

They are not plot agents. They do not deliver information Jean needs. They show up
in places where he can breathe for a moment, and that is the whole of their purpose,
which is not a small thing.

The secondary throughline is **the Liss and Gorran relationship**, which builds
across every encounter. Each meeting adds one layer — a small thing, rarely spoken,
always felt. The relationship should never be announced, explained, or summarized
in the prose. It is demonstrated only. By the final encounter, the player
understands what these two are to each other without having been told.

---

## Where They Travel

Mara's trading routes take her group through specific territories. Devet and Liss
go where Mara goes. Their reappearances are plausible because:

- The river crossing is on any route between the eastern mountain country and
  the western territories — a natural waypoint
- The approaches to the Wailing Badlands are passed through by traders and
  scavengers moving between the Resolute Plains and the western caves
- The Resolute Plains are primary nomad territory; all nomad groups pass through
- Later chapters bring Jean to territories that are also on Mara's longer circuits

They are not following Jean. They are going the same direction for their own reasons.

## Encounter 1 — East Bank River Camp
*Chapter 2 → 3 Transition. First meeting.*

Documented in full in `docs/lore/story/ch02-ch03-transition.md` and referenced in
`docs/lore/character-profiles/mara.md`. This is the baseline.

**Liss and Gorran:**
Liss circles Gorran's general area without approaching. She asks Mara questions
about Golemites in a low voice that Gorran can certainly hear. She asks Gorran
directly, once — something simple and curious. He looks at her. He does not answer.
She asks something else. He still doesn't answer.

She is not frightened by this. She accepts it and sits near him and watches the
river instead. He allows this. Nothing is exchanged. It is the warmest moment
in the sequence.

**What is established:**
- Liss's curiosity about Gorran is immediate and total
- Gorran's response: patient allowance, nothing more
- The dynamic is fixed at parallel observation — she watches him, he permits it
- Devet watches this from the fire, says nothing, refills his cup

**Devet and Jean:**
Devet gives Jean one look at arrival: the look of a man who knows what the look
of someone running toward something doesn't look like. He does not offer this
observation aloud. He offers food.

Jean takes the food. Devet returns to the fire.

## Encounter 2 — Badlands Approach Camp
*Early Chapter 3. Optional.*

The nomad group is camped on the western approach — the dry, broken terrain east
of the Wailing Badlands proper. Mara has been here before, scavenging the outer
approach for artifacts left by people who turned back. Devet has made camp in the
hollow where the wind is manageable.

Jean and Gorran, moving west, find the camp before they expect to. This is not
a plot encounter. It is a fire and someone cooking.

**Liss and Gorran — The Stone:**

Liss has found something on the approach terrain: a piece of mineral that has been
fractured in a specific way, leaving a surface that catches light differently than
ordinary rock. It is interesting. She has been carrying it.

She does not talk at Gorran this time. She has learned, from the river camp,
that talking at Gorran does not produce the results she's looking for. She
approaches instead with no preamble — just herself and the stone.

She holds the stone out. Flat on her palm. She says nothing.

Gorran looks at the stone. Not at her — at the stone. His attention on it is
complete, unhurried. He studies it the way he studies everything: as a material
object with properties worth understanding.

Then he takes it.

He holds it. He tilts it in the light. He runs a thumb across the fractured face.
He is not performing this — he is doing it. He sets it down carefully between them,
not handing it back, not keeping it. Setting it in the space they share.

Liss looks at the stone. She looks at him. She sits down.

They sit beside the stone together for a while.

**What this adds:**
- Gorran takes something from her hand — the first real acknowledgment
- The stone is set between them, not returned — a shared object, not a gift rejected
- Liss's learning: she was trying words; she switches to objects; the object works
- No one comments on this

**Devet:**
Devet has been watching from behind the fire. He refills his cup. He is smiling —
one of his small, interior events.

He catches Mara's eye. He does not say anything. She returns to her sorting.

**Devet and Jean:**
Jean sits at the fire longer this time. He is tired in a way that predates sleep.
Devet produces a bowl and does not hand it to Jean so much as set it in Jean's
field of reach, where Jean will notice it eventually.

At some point Jean says something — about the road, about what's ahead, or nothing
in particular. Devet listens. He does not respond at length. He says one thing
that is brief and plainly true. Then he puts more wood on the fire.

Jean will carry what Devet said with him for a while without being able to explain why.

## Encounter 3 — Resolute Plains Waypoint
*Chapter 4. The group is crossing or has recently crossed the Resolute Plains.*

The Resolute Plains are primary nomad territory. Mara's routes run through here.
Jean encounters the group at a recognized waypoint — a crossroads of sorts, a place
where nomad camps have been made in the same spot for longer than any individual
nomad can remember.

The plains are wide, exposed, and — for Jean, whose journey has been spent in caves,
mountains, and badlands — disorienting in their openness. The camp at the waypoint
is the first structure-with-a-fire he has seen in some time.

Devet is cooking. He has been cooking for a while by the time Jean arrives.
This is reassuring in the way that only ordinary things can be reassuring.

**Liss and Gorran — Stop:**

Gorran is now at Stage 1 of his language arc. He has "Stop." He has used it once,
in an emergency with Jean. He has used nothing else.

Liss, on the plains, has found a series of interesting formations — fractured rock
pillars eroded into shapes that she has given names and a provisional mythology.
She is investigating one that is less stable than it looks.

Gorran, who has been watching the plains perimeter (a habit, not an assignment),
sees this.

He says: **"Stop."**

Not loudly. Not urgently, in the way he said it to Jean. Plainly. One word,
the same word, used in a slightly different key.

Liss stops. She looks back at him.

He is looking at the pillar, not at her. He makes a gesture — the load-bearing
kind, not decorative — indicating she should come away from it.

She comes. The pillar creaks slightly in the wind.

She walks back to where Gorran is standing. She looks up at him — he is very much
larger than her, which is a fact she has not previously been close enough to register
at full scale. She considers this fact without alarm.

> "You can talk."

He looks at her.

> "I mean — I wondered. After the river."

He looks at the pillar, which is settling.

> "Can you say my name? It's Liss."

He does not. He looks back at the plains.

She watches him not say her name. She files this: he understands more than he says.
The gap between what Gorran understands and what he produces — she has just located it.

This changes everything about how she approaches him from here on.

She sits beside him and watches the plains. After a moment she says:

> "That's all right. I'll figure out what you can say."

Gorran says nothing. This is, in its way, an agreement.

**What this adds:**
- Gorran uses "Stop" for Liss — the same word he used for Jean in an emergency,
  deployed here in a small protective act. The word works differently at this scale.
- Liss discovers the comprehension-production gap: he understands; he simply doesn't speak
- The protective instinct is now established — he is not just allowing her attention,
  he is attending to her
- Her project shifts: she stops asking questions he can't answer and starts trying
  to find the ones he can

**Devet and Jean:**

Jean has been on the plains long enough to have lost some of the badlands in him.
He looks lighter than Encounter 2. Not fine — lighter.

Devet notices this in the way he notices things: without comment, in the first second.

Later, when Jean is sitting at the fire and doing the thing where he is present but
not quite present, Devet says:

> *"River crossing in three days if you're heading north. Different guide —
> a man named Ossel, younger, good enough. He'll charge more."*

A pause.

> *"Tell him Devet sent you. He'll charge the same."*

This is the whole of it. Jean will find Ossel's crossing useful later. Devet
already knows this, in the low-key way he knows most things.

## Encounter 4 — Eastern Plains Camp
*Chapter 4, later. The nomad group is camped a few days east of the Wayfarers territory.*

This encounter is shorter than the others. Jean passes through. Mara is busy with
something. Devet is cooking.

Liss has a project.

**Liss and Gorran — Stone:**

Gorran is now building Stage 2. His vocabulary is beginning to expand, one word at a
time. He does not always show this. He shows it to Jean, occasionally, in the moments
it matters. He has not yet shown it to Liss.

Liss has decided she is teaching him words.

The methodology is direct: she holds up an object, says its name, waits. Then holds
up the next object. She has assembled a small collection of camp materials for
this purpose: a wooden spoon, a cup, a stone, a feather.

She has been doing this for several minutes. Gorran has been watching. He has not
produced any of the words.

She holds up the stone.

> "Stone."

Gorran looks at her. He looks at the stone. He looks back at her.

> "Stone."

Liss goes absolutely still.

Then:

> "MARA."

From across camp, not looking up: *"I heard."*

Devet, from the fire, not looking up: *"He's been listening since you started."*

Gorran says nothing. He has spent his one word.

Liss looks at him. She looks at the stone. She sets it down between them — the
same gesture he used for her stone at the Badlands approach, which she has been
thinking about since. She does not say anything.

After a moment she holds up the feather.

He watches her. He does not say "feather." But he is watching.

**What this adds:**
- Gorran produces a word for Liss — "Stone," one of his Stage 2 acquisitions,
  deployed here for her
- The stone-between-them callback: she returns his gesture
- She continues the teaching project without requiring immediate results
- The relationship has solidified into something mutual without being announced:
  she has a project for him, he has a word for her

**Devet:**

Devet has, at some point in this encounter, produced a meal that is marginally nicer
than usual. There is no obvious reason for this. He does not explain it.

Jean, if he notices, is welcome to form his own conclusions.

## Encounter 5 — Late Game
*Chapter 5 or end of Chapter 4. Specific location to be determined by the chapter structure.*

This is the deepest encounter. It may follow a significant story beat — something
that has cost Jean something. The camp should feel like it materialized specifically
to be where he needed it. It didn't. They were going this direction anyway.

**Liss and Gorran — Here. Now.**

Gorran is approaching Stage 3. He has short phrases — subject-verb, no articles.
He uses them with Jean in functional contexts. He has not used them with Liss.

It is late. The others are asleep or occupied. Gorran is sitting with the particular
quality of stillness that is not rest but keeping watch. Liss is sitting near him —
her default position now, established by repetition into something like a fact.

She asks him something. It is one of her real questions — the ones she has been
sitting with since the river camp, that she has not asked because they are too large.

> "Where do you come from? Like — where exactly?"

A long pause. The fire.

> "Stone."

She sits with this.

> "That sounds lonely."

He is quiet. He is looking at the fire. He looks at her.

> "Here. Now."

She considers this.

> "Yeah. Here now."

She pulls her knees up and watches the fire. He watches the perimeter. This is
the whole of it. It is enough.

**What this means:**
- Gorran's "Here. Now." is not a sentence he has used before. It is not from
  Jean's vocabulary in the way most of his words are. He has assembled it himself,
  from the fragments he has — *here* (directional, Stage 2), *now* (temporal,
  acquired through Jean's habit of saying "right now" when working) — and used
  them together to say: *the loneliness was then; this is not then.*
- He does not say this in full. He says it in two words. She hears the full sentence.
- "Yeah. Here now." is her answer: she receives it, reflects it back, and that is
  the bond named. No further naming is required.

**Devet and Jean:**

Devet has been watching this from where he sits. He says nothing. He does not look
at Jean. But after a moment he sets a bowl near Jean's feet — not handed over,
just placed within reach.

Jean picks up the bowl.

This is the last time he will see Devet and Liss before the end of the game.
He doesn't know this yet. Neither does Devet. But Devet has a sense for these
things, and the bowl is slightly larger than usual.

## Pacing and Tonal Notes

**Between encounters:**

Nothing should be said in subsequent encounters about what happened in the previous
one. The accumulation is in the behavior, not the recap. Gorran does not acknowledge
previous meetings with Liss in words. She does not reference them with him either.
The history is expressed in the distance she sits from him (closer each time) and
the direction he positions himself relative to her (between her and whatever requires
attention, consistently, without announcement).

**The humor:**

Devet's dry observations are scattered, not concentrated. Any single encounter should
have at most one — the one that lands. His humor requires space. If he says something
funny every time Jean sits at his fire, the timing stops working.

Liss's comedy is constant but not slapstick. She is funny because she says the true
thing without the social conditioning that would have softened or suppressed it. The
humor is in the unguarded honesty of it, not in the situation. She should never be
written as a comic relief character — she is a character who is frequently funny,
which is a different thing.

**Gorran and Liss together:**

Never write their dynamic with warmth telegraphed ahead of the moment. The narration
does not explain what their proximity means. It describes what is happening — where
they are sitting, what Gorran is attending to, whether she has brought something —
and lets the player arrive at the meaning. This is especially critical for Encounter 5.

**Jean's relationship to these encounters:**

He does not need to understand what Liss and Gorran are becoming to each other.
He may register it the way he registers most things in the background of his
bigger grief — obliquely, partially, more feeling than thought. If the prose gives
him a reaction to it, the reaction should be quiet: a beat, a look, then he
continues.

The encounters are for Jean. They are not about Jean. This is their value.

## Implementation Notes

- Devet and Liss require NPC classes (see `ch02-ch03-transition.md` — already noted).
  Encounters 2–5 require them to be present without necessarily being interactable
  in every encounter; some appearances may be narrated scene-setting with Mara as
  the interaction point.
- Encounter 3's "Stop" scene requires careful coordination with Gorran's language arc
  (Stage 1). Do not write it before Stage 1 has been established with Jean.
- Encounter 4's "Stone" scene requires Gorran to be in early Stage 2. It should not
  occur in the same chapter as the first "Stop" — there should be enough travel time
  between encounters for the vocabulary expansion to feel earned.
- Encounter 5's "Here. Now." requires Stage 3. This is the emotional peak of the
  relationship and should occur late — after Jean's major arc beat in Chapter 4 or 5.
- The stone that Gorran takes in Encounter 2 should persist as a prop detail: if the
  player sees Gorran's inventory or movement description at any subsequent point, the
  stone may appear. He kept it. He did not make a ceremony of this.
- Devet's "Tell him Devet sent you" (Encounter 3) should have a practical downstream:
  Ossel should exist as a minor NPC or reference point in the Resolute Plains territory.
