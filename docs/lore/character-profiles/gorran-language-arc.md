# Gorran — Language Acquisition Arc: Narrative Notes

Gorran is 1,500 years old and has never had reason to learn a human language.
He begins the game unable to speak to Jean at all. Over the course of their time
together, he learns. He never becomes fluent. The game ends before he could.

This matters for one specific scene: the crisis, where Gorran sits beside Jean in the
dark and says something that costs him everything he has linguistically. Those words
need to be earned. They will not land unless the player has watched him build toward
them, word by word, for the entire game.

---

## The Starting State

### What Gorran Can Do
Gorran communicates through:

- **Physical gesture.** He places himself. He moves in front of things. He puts a
  hand on Jean's shoulder or on his chest (not gently — it's a weight, an anchor).
  He points. He blocks. He does not wave or gesture expressively; his movements are
  load-bearing, not decorative.
- **Sound.** A low sustained rumble means warning or attention. A short sharp crack
  means urgency or alarm. A subsonic vibration Jean feels in his sternum rather than
  hears — this is Gorran's presence-sound, the closest thing he has to "I'm here."
  Over time Jean learns to read these before he learns Gorran's words.
- **Stillness.** Gorran's version of a strong opinion is to simply not move. Jean
  wants to go somewhere; Gorran doesn't move from his path. The argument is physical.

### What Gorran Understands vs. What He Can Produce
Gorran's receptive language develops faster than his productive language, as with any
learner. By mid-game he understands most of what Jean says. By late game he understands
nearly everything. His productive vocabulary always lags behind. There will be moments
where Jean says something complex and Gorran responds with a single word or gesture
that makes clear he understood all of it. This gap — between comprehension and expression —
is part of his character throughout.

### How the Narration Renders Early Communication
In Stage 0, Gorran has no spoken lines. His communication is entirely narrated:

> *Gorran steps in front of the passage. He does not move.*
> *A low vibration moves through the stone floor.*
> *Gorran places one hand on your shoulder — briefly, then withdraws it.*

The player reads Gorran before they hear him.

---

## Why He Learns

Gorran spent centuries in the Great Quiet. Centuries standing watch alone, no
companions, the only sounds the slow trickle of water and the grinding of the
stone he was guarding. He is not incapable of learning human language; he has simply
never had the immersion. He has heard human speech before — Golemites have interacted
with humans throughout their history — but isolated encounters don't build a language.

What Jean gives Gorran is something he has not had since the Great Quiet ended: one
voice, sustained, over time.

Jean talks constantly. HVAC techs narrate their work — tracing problems aloud, talking
through what they're doing, muttering diagnostics at the thing they're fixing. Jean
does this in Aurelion without knowing he's doing it. He also talks to himself in other
ways: half-finished thoughts, the occasional instruction to no one, words that are
prayers that have stopped being addressed to anyone in particular.

Gorran listens. He has spent centuries learning to be extraordinarily still and patient.
Listening is what the Great Quiet taught him. Jean has given him something to listen to.

## The Acquisition Arc

### Stage 0 — No Words
*Corresponds to: Denial → Anger (early to mid game)*

No spoken lines. All narrated gesture and sound. Gorran's presence is felt before it is
heard. Jean learns his physical vocabulary — the different qualities of rumble, the meaning
of the stillness, the weight of the hand — before any word is exchanged.

Jean probably wonders, at some point, whether Gorran is capable of speech. He does not ask.
He is not sure he wants to know.

---

### Stage 1 — First Word
*Corresponds to: Anger / early Bargaining*

The first word arrives in an emergency.

Jean is about to make a decision Gorran cannot physically stop in time — or Gorran is
across a space and needs to reach him faster than his body allows. He does the one thing
he has not done yet.

**The first word is "Stop."**

Not Jean's name. Not a greeting. A directive — the first word because it is the most
urgent, the one Gorran has been building toward without knowing he was building toward it.
He has heard Jean say it (to himself, to the situation, to the problem in front of him)
enough times to have it ready.

It comes out slightly wrong. The vowel is flat, the consonants too hard, the word
shaped by a mouth that has never made these sounds before. But it is unmistakably
a human word, in a human language, in Jean's language.

Jean stops. Then he stands there for a moment.

> *Gorran is looking at you. He does not explain.*

He doesn't explain because he can't yet. He said the one word he had. That is all.

From this point Gorran does not immediately add more words. Stage 1 is a single word —
or occasionally two — used only when gesture and sound are insufficient. He conserves
language like someone who knows exactly how much they have.

---

### Stage 2 — Expanding Single Words
*Corresponds to: Bargaining*

Gorran's vocabulary grows slowly. The words he acquires are the words Jean says most
frequently, the ones with the clearest functional meaning in context.

**Words acquired in Stage 2 (rough order):**
- "Stop" — already has it
- "Here" / "There" — directional, essential
- "Wait" — slightly different from stop; he learns the distinction
- "Good" — Jean says this when something works; Gorran hears it as an assessment
- "No" — he understands this long before he uses it; when he uses it, it carries weight
- Jean's name — he attempts this quietly first, slightly mispronounced, then closer

The first time Gorran says Jean's name correctly — not approximately but correctly —
is a specific moment worth marking. Jean hears it and doesn't immediately respond.
Then he does.

In combat during Stage 2, Gorran's lines are still primarily narrated action, but
single-word interruptions begin:

> *Gorran swings and the creature is hurled back.*
> "Back!" — at you.

The player may not be certain, at first, whether it was a word or just a sound.

---

### Stage 3 — Short Phrases
*Corresponds to: Bargaining → the Breaking*

Gorran begins combining words. The grammar is stripped to the minimum: subject-verb,
no articles, minimal prepositions, present tense by default.

> "Jean. Come."
> "Not safe. Here."
> "I hold."
> "Passage. Weight. Wrong."

That last one is characteristic of Stage 3: Gorran's thought-patterns show through
the acquired words. He doesn't say "there's something in the passage." He describes
it as a material property — weight, texture, temperature — because that is how Golemites
perceive the world. His sentences are structured like observations from a body made
of stone: what does the stone feel? What is the weight? What is the texture?

This applies to emotional content too. He doesn't say "I am sad." He says things like:
- "Heavy." (when something is sorrowful)
- "Cold." (when something is frightening)
- "Rough." (when he is angry, or distressed)

Jean may not always understand these, but the player begins to.

In combat, Stage 3 produces genuine functional exchanges:

> "Jean — behind. Two."
> "I take left. You go."
> "Stand. I am here."

The shorthand is beginning. They don't need many words for this.

---

### Stage 4 — Effortful Sentences
*Corresponds to: The Breaking / Depression*

By the time of the crisis scene, Gorran has enough to form a complete sentence. Not
comfortably. It costs him. When he says something of emotional complexity in Stage 4,
the prose should convey the effort: the pauses, the reaching, the imperfection of
a mind that thinks in stone trying to say something in the shape of grief.

He still has no articles. Past tense is present but effortful — he reaches for it when
he needs it, doesn't always find it. Emotional weight is still rendered as physical
property, but now alongside more direct language.

**The crisis scene lines (revised for Stage 4):**

After Gorran has sat beside Jean in silence for a long time, he says:

> "Earth takes. I have not... understood why."

He stops. He is looking at the stone, not at Jean.

> "Was angry. For long time."

A pause.

> "Then not angry. Do not know if... better."

He does not look at Jean when he finishes. He has used everything he had.

This is not eloquent. It is not meant to be. Its power is precisely that it is the
most Gorran has ever said, shaped by language he built from nothing over the course
of the game, reaching past the limit of his vocabulary to say something he needs
Jean to hear. The imperfection is not a flaw. It is the weight of the thing.

---

## The Persistent Character of Gorran's Speech

At every stage, even Stage 4, Gorran's language has a consistent texture:

- **No articles.** "The" and "a" are absent or used rarely and incorrectly. He says
  "passage" not "the passage," "you" not "are you."
- **Present tense by default.** Past tense is effortful and only used when the
  distinction matters. "Was angry" costs more than "angry."
- **Physical vocabulary for emotional content.** He does not say he feels things.
  He describes material states: heavy, cold, rough, still. The player learns to read
  this alongside Jean.
- **Pauses rendered in text as "..."** — mid-sentence, when reaching for a word.
  Not hesitation. Weight.
- **Short sentences by preference.** He says what he needs to say and stops. He does
  not fill silence. He never learned to.
- **No idioms.** He learned from Jean, and Jean doesn't use idioms when working or
  when grieving. Gorran's vocabulary is functional, not decorative.

## Combat Flavor by Stage

| Stage | Format | Example Lines |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Narrated only | *Gorran bellows — a sound like two boulders shearing.* / *A crack, sharp and close.* |
| 1 | Narrated + rare single word | *"Back!"* (Jean may not be certain he heard it) |
| 2 | Narrated + single-word directions | *"Jean — behind."* / *"No."* / *"Here."* |
| 3 | Short functional exchanges | *"I take left."* / *"Stand. I hold them."* / *"Done."* |
| 4 | Shorthand, brief, earned | *"I see it."* / *"Go. I follow."* / *"Good."* (said rarely, landing hard) |

Stage 4 combat lines are sparse by design. By then Jean and Gorran have a shorthand
that barely needs language. The fewer words Gorran says in battle, the more each one
means.

---

## The Word He Uses for Jean

Gorran's version of Jean's name is worth a note. In Stage 1 he approximates it —
slightly wrong vowel, slightly too much weight on the consonant. In Stage 2 he corrects
himself. By Stage 3 he says it correctly. He does not say it often. When he does, Jean
hears the effort that went into learning it.

Gorran never uses Jean's name lightly. When he says it in combat, Jean pays attention.
When he says it outside of combat, it means something specific: *I am talking to you,
not around you.* It is the closest Gorran gets to emphasis.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Do not let Gorran become fluent.** The game ends before this would be possible.
  He should end the game as a clear Stage 4 speaker — capable of saying something
  meaningful but never gracefully, never easily.
- **Do not have Gorran produce long speeches.** Even in Stage 4, more than three or
  four sentences is too many. The crisis lines are the maximum. Everything after that
  should be shorter.
- **Do not let the words arrive without cost.** The effort should always be visible.
  When Gorran says something emotionally complex, the prose marks the pauses.
- **Do not have Gorran use abstractions he couldn't have learned from Jean.** His
  vocabulary is built from one voice. He doesn't know words Jean doesn't use.
- **Do not have Gorran understand more than Jean has said.** He understands Jean's
  words; he does not have access to Jean's thoughts or memories.
- **The first word must be earned.** Do not have Gorran speak early. The silence
  before "Stop" is what makes it land.
