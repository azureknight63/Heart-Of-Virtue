# Liss — Character Profile

## Overview

Liss is young — old enough to carry her share of camp work and young enough that
she hasn't yet learned which questions are rude to ask. She has been traveling
with Mara and Devet long enough that nomadic life is the only life she remembers,
which means she has no concept of walls as a permanent feature of existence and
has never understood why people stay in one place past the point it gets interesting.

She has an opinion about everything and a filter for none of it. This is not a
flaw she is working on correcting. She does not yet know it is considered a flaw.

She is funny in a way she doesn't intend and honest in a way she doesn't weaponize.
She asks the questions that everyone else has quietly decided not to ask. Sometimes
this is illuminating. Sometimes it is alarming. Usually it is both, in the same breath.

She collects things. Not valuably — not like Mara, whose collection has a system
and a commercial logic. Liss collects whatever is interesting to her: an unusually
colored stone, a feather with a bent shaft, a scrap of something whose origin she
has invented a story about. She has to leave most of it behind when camp moves.
She does, without much grief. New interesting things will present themselves.

---

## Physical Appearance and Manner

Liss is at the age where she has grown into herself recently enough that she still
sometimes moves like someone slightly smaller. She is quick and direct — she does
not approach things sideways. She approaches them directly, often at a pace that
is not quite running but is faster than most adults find comfortable.

She is usually carrying something. It is usually a stone.

Her expressions are immediate and unconcealed. Whatever she is thinking is visible
before she has decided whether to share it. She has not learned to decide first.

She is not loud, exactly, but she occupies her full presence. A room — or a campsite
— adjusts slightly when she arrives.

---

## What She Is Curious About

Everything, with no reliable order of priority. But recurring subjects:

**Golemites.** She has heard stories but not had access to one until Gorran. She
has questions that range from the practical (what does stone feel like from inside?
does it hurt when you're cracked?) to the philosophical (do Golemites dream? do they
remember things differently if they can't write them down?) to the ones she has
invented herself and wants confirmed (are the stars just the light coming up through
old Golemite eyes that went dark?). She does not expect all of these to be answered.
She asks them anyway, into the general vicinity of Gorran, without much regard for
whether she is being heard.

**Jean.** Something about him doesn't fit together, and she is the only one who
asks about this directly. Not cruelly — she is never cruel, she simply doesn't have
the social architecture for cruelty yet — but plainly. *You look like you're dressed
for a different story. Is that your actual mace or did you find it? Where are you
going that you have to look like that?* She does not always get answers. She
considers this data.

**The mace specifically.** She finds religious objects fascinating in the way she
finds all objects with a visible past fascinating. She has asked about it more than
once. She has not gotten a satisfying answer. She is still working on this.

**Where things come from and where they are going.** She thinks about routes
differently than adults do — not as logistics but as stories. Every path leads
somewhere someone decided to go. She wants to know who decided and why.

---

## What She Brings to Jean

Liss interacts with Jean in a way no other NPC does: she is not reading his grief,
not watching him from lateral attention, not offering wisdom or competence. She
is simply curious about him as a specific person who has shown up in her camp and
has a mace with a story she hasn't gotten to the bottom of yet.

This is, paradoxically, a relief.

Jean's defenses are calibrated against people who want something from his pain —
to understand it, to witness it, to help with it. Liss doesn't want any of those
things. She wants to know if the mace is heavy and what the inscription means and
whether he's going somewhere dangerous and if so whether he knows what plants are
edible on that route because Devet does and she could ask.

She is too young to be condescending. She has no investment in Jean's identity.
She does not need him to be the crusader or the man who has lost his faith or the
person carrying grief. She just needs him to be the person currently sitting at the
fire, which he is, which is enough.

Her moments with Jean are brief, unexpected, and — in the way of encounters with
children who haven't learned to be diplomatic — occasionally more honest than he
was prepared for.

---

## The Gorran Project

From the moment Liss sees Gorran, he becomes a subject of serious inquiry.

She has never been this close to a Golemite. She has heard about them — Mara has
told her what she knows, which is structural fact, not texture. Liss wants the
texture. She approaches the question the way she approaches all difficult objects:
by circling the outside until she understands the shape, then moving in.

This is a project. She is patient about projects.

**What makes Gorran interesting to her (beyond the obvious):**

- He is old in a way she can't calculate. She has learned that Devet is old, and
  what old looks like on a human. Gorran's age doesn't read the same way. It is
  everywhere and nowhere — it doesn't make him slow or diminished, it just means
  there is more of him than she can see.
- He doesn't answer questions. This is unusual. Most people answer, even if the
  answer is a deflection. Gorran doesn't deflect; he simply doesn't answer, which
  means the question stays in the air. She finds herself thinking about them later.
- He allows her attention without requiring anything of it. She has never had
  someone simply permit her to look before. Most people, when watched too directly,
  do something — shift, speak, smile, move away. Gorran does none of these things.
  He is there, and she may look, and that is all. She finds this unexpectedly restful.

Over time, across multiple encounters, her curiosity about him becomes something
more settled. The project changes: she stops needing to understand Golemites in
general and starts simply wanting to be where Gorran is, which is a different
thing entirely. She doesn't have a name for this distinction. She arrives at it
by feel.

---

## How She Relates to Devet

Devet is the bedrock of her world and she does not know this because she has
never lived in a world without him. He is the person who was there before she
had a self to remember with. He is the fire that is always the right temperature.

She talks at him constantly. He listens. He responds with his name-call when
she goes too far, and she hears everything he means by it.

She saves the interesting things she finds for him first. He examines them
without performance. He says "yes" or "not that one" and she trusts his judgment
on the second entirely and ignores it on the first, which he expects.

She does not worry about Devet in the way she sometimes worries about Mara
(who goes into places that look dangerous) or Jean (who looks like someone
doing something very difficult). Devet is not the kind of person you worry about.
He is the kind of person who worries about other people, slowly and without
telling them.

---

## How She Relates to Mara

Mara is the horizon — always slightly ahead, always going somewhere Liss is
going to follow. Liss admires her in the absolute, uncomplicated way of someone
who has decided that this is what competence looks like and she intends to be
that someday.

Mara answers her questions when she has the answer and says "I don't know" when
she doesn't and never invents an answer to fill the gap. Liss has noticed this.
She has filed it under *how to be*.

She has also noticed that Mara watches people the way she watches artifacts: for
what they tell her about what they are and where they've been. She tries to do
this too. She is not good at it yet. She tells Mara this. Mara says she's better
at it than she thinks. Liss is still working out whether this is true.

---

## Voice and Speech

Liss speaks at the pace of her thinking, which is faster than most conversations.
She asks follow-up questions before the initial question has been answered. She
corrects herself mid-sentence. She has started and abandoned more sentences than
she has finished.

She is not rude. She is not unkind. She is simply running at full speed without
a governor.

**Voice examples:**

> *"What does the inscription on your mace mean? The bit near the grip —
> that part specifically. Mara said it might be ceremonial but it looks
> functional to me. Is it functional? Can I see it?"*

---

> *"You're stone. Does that mean the cold feels different? Because Devet
> says the cold settles in his bones and I thought, if you're already bone
> basically, does it feel like — does it feel like anything? Or is it just
> cold the same as everything else?"*
>
> *Gorran looks at her.*
>
> *"...You don't have to answer."*
>
> *Gorran continues looking at the fire.*
>
> *"I'll probably ask again sometime."*

---

> *Jean, after something difficult:* "I'm fine."
>
> *Liss, looking at him:* "You look like Devet after the crossing where
> the raft hit the rock. He said he was fine then too."
>
> *A pause.*
>
> *"He made soup."*

## What to Avoid

- **Do not make her precocious in a knowing way.** She is curious, not wise.
  She stumbles onto truths because she has no idea they're there, not because
  she is shrewd. She does not deliver epiphanies. She asks questions that happen
  to illuminate things.
- **Do not give her a tragic backstory.** She is not defined by loss. She is
  light, genuinely. This is not naïveté waiting to be corrected — it is a
  specific quality she has. The game does not need to take it from her.
- **Do not have her grow up in scene.** Her development across encounters is
  subtle — a little more steady, a little more present. Not the shedding of
  childhood but the gradual accumulation of someone she is becoming. She remains
  recognizably herself.
- **Do not have her ask about Jean's grief directly.** She asks about what she
  sees: the mace, the bandage, whether he knows which plants are safe. She does
  not identify emotional states and ask about them. She has not learned to do that.
  The questions she does ask sometimes brush the grief sideways.
- **Do not accelerate the Gorran relationship.** It builds at the speed of trust
  between a very old stone thing and a very young human — which is to say, slowly,
  through small moments, without being announced.
- **Do not let her become Gorran's translator.** She is not a tool for making him
  legible. She is a person becoming attached to him. These are different.
