# Devet — Character Profile

## Overview

Devet is Mara's uncle — the exact word is "uncle" in practice, though the
genealogy behind it has never been explained and neither of them would think
to volunteer it. He travels with her loose group as a matter of long habit.
He was doing this before Mara was born. He will probably be doing it after.

He does the cooking. He does not do much else that requires comment.

He is not a minor character in the sense of someone whose presence doesn't
register. He registers. He registers quietly, without effort, in the way that
very settled people tend to. A fire burns more evenly when Devet is tending it.
A camp sits more naturally in its landscape when he's the one who chose the site.
These things are felt before they're understood.

He is funny in a way that requires close attention to catch. He doesn't perform it.
He doesn't set it up. He says one thing, the minimum possible thing, and the comedy
is in what he left out. People who don't know him miss it entirely. People who know
him catch it and don't always laugh — they look at him, and he is already attending
to the fire.

---

## Physical Appearance and Manner

Devet is old in a way that doesn't invite estimation. He has been old for a long
time and has clearly decided this is fine. He is not diminished by it. His movements
are deliberate and economical — nothing wasted, nothing hurried. He has the body
of someone who has walked a great deal of terrain over a great many years and has
arrived at a relationship with distance that is beyond ambivalence.

He dresses in layers that have been repaired more times than they have been replaced.
He carries what he needs. His cookware is battered and extremely well-maintained.

His expression at rest is not stern but settled. He does not look like a man waiting
for something. He looks like a man who has stopped waiting, which is different.

When he smiles, it is a small, interior event that mostly happens in the corners of
his eyes. He does not announce it. It may or may not be for anyone present.

---

## Occupation and Role

Devet's primary function in the group is cooking. This is not a lesser role.
He has elevated it to a form of fluency — he knows what people need from food
before they do, and delivers it without being asked. In cold weather, the bowl
arrives at the right moment. In grief, the portion is larger and the seasoning
simpler. He does not explain this calculation. He makes no reference to it.

Beyond cooking, he maintains the camp. He reads weather. He knows the plains and
the river country as well as Mara knows the cavern approaches — from long years of
travel rather than specialized study. He knows which plants are edible when the
usual food runs low. He knows where old paths run that aren't on any map because
they haven't been maps for longer than most people's grandfathers have been alive.

He occasionally produces an old story — something he remembers from a time before
the current era, some fragment of knowledge from a source he never names. He does
this without announcement, without context, and without explanation. The relevance
usually becomes apparent later. He does not draw attention to this when it does.

---

## What He Has Seen

Devet has lived long enough that the river crossing where Jean first meets him
is a crossing he has made, at a rough estimate, several hundred times. He has
seen the look of people heading west before. He knows what urgency looks like,
what despair looks like, what someone walking toward something looks like. He
catalogues these at a glance and offers no commentary.

He has watched the world change around him with the mild attention of a man
watching weather. He has outlived several eras of his own travel companions.
He does not bring this up. He has buried his dead and continued walking. The
continued walking is the whole of his philosophy, to the extent he has one.

He finds people endlessly comprehensible. Not admirable, not pitiful — comprehensible.
He has seen enough human behavior that very little surprises him, and he regards
what doesn't surprise him with something close to affection. Even the desperate ones.
Especially the desperate ones.

This is where the humor lives: in the gap between what people think they're hiding
and what is obvious to someone who has been watching for this long. He does not
expose this gap. He feeds people through it.

---

## How He Relates to Mara

Mara is his sister's daughter. He has traveled with her since she was old enough
to carry her own pack, which she insisted on earlier than was sensible. He has
watched her become extremely good at everything she set her hand to and has observed
this with the quiet satisfaction of someone who expected nothing less and is pleased
to have been right.

He does not advise her. She does not ask. He does not need to. The dynamic resolved
itself years ago into something more comfortable than counsel: she leads, he follows,
and if he has a view he holds it in reserve and deploys it once, plainly, when it
is most needed. She listens then. She does not always say so.

He teases her. Not often, not sharply — a dry observation, perfectly timed. She
usually doesn't react. He doesn't require her to.

He has been Mara since before Mara was Mara. He knows what she's built from.

---

## How He Relates to Liss

Liss fills every silence Devet doesn't need filled, and he doesn't mind. She asks
questions he has already answered, she collects things she will have to leave behind,
and she approaches the world with a confidence that has not yet been taught caution.
He finds this correct. He expects it to last exactly as long as it should.

He does not lecture her. He does not caution her against things that haven't
happened yet. He watches. If she goes too far he says one word — her name, no
inflection — and she hears the full sentence in it because she has learned to.

He feeds her first. This is the whole of his parental instinct expressed in
practice.

He is aware of what is happening between her and Gorran — more aware, probably,
than Mara, who observes it as an artifact of interesting behavior. Devet watches
it with something that might be recognition. He says nothing about it because
nothing needs to be said. It is going to be what it's going to be. He will be
here when she needs to tell someone.

---

## How He Relates to Jean

Jean is not a puzzle to Devet. He is a type Devet has seen before: someone who
is moving because stopping is worse, carrying something he has not looked at
directly, wearing an identity that fits less well than it used to. The details
are different from previous examples. The shape is familiar.

Devet offers Jean food. This is not a gesture. It is an assessment: *you are a
person who needs to eat, and I am a person who can address that.* Everything else
is secondary.

He does not ask Jean about himself. He does not ask Jean's companions about
him. He makes room at the fire and leaves it at that.

Over subsequent encounters, a small warmth develops — not friendship, not the
dynamic Jean has with Mara. Something older-feeling than that. The sort of
regard that comes from a man who has seen many people cross rivers and has
decided this one was worth watching.

At some point, Devet says something to Jean that is brief and plainly true and
that Jean will not forget. He will say it as though it is obvious, because to
him it is. He will not elaborate. He will offer more soup.

---

## Speech and Voice

Devet speaks rarely and briefly. He is not terse in the way of someone who distrusts
words — he simply doesn't use more than a situation requires.

When he does speak, the words land. He has an old person's precision with language:
he has tried many ways of saying things and has arrived at the one that works.

His humor is delivered without any of the markers of humor. No timing pause, no
register change, no waiting for a reaction. He says the thing and continues what
he was doing. This is what makes it funny.

**Voice examples:**

> *(Jean, having returned from something difficult):* "Soup's ready."
>
> *(Jean starting to decline):* "Soup."
>
> *Jean eats the soup.*

---

> *Jean: "Has she always been like this?"* *(re: Liss)*
>
> *Devet, not looking up:* "Since she could talk."
>
> *A pause.*
>
> *"Before that, probably."*

---

> *Someone asking Devet how long he's been traveling with Mara:*
>
> *"Long enough that I don't remember what I was doing before."*
>
> *He considers.*
>
> *"Probably something less useful."*

---

> *(After Gorran takes something from Liss's outstretched hand — the first real
> acknowledgment):*
>
> *Devet watches. He refills his cup. He says nothing.*
>
> *He is smiling.*

---

## What to Avoid

- **Do not make him wise in a dispensing-wisdom way.** His insight lives in what
  he observes and says plainly, not in aphorisms. He is not a mentor. He is a
  man who has been paying attention for a very long time.
- **Do not give him backstory in exposition.** What he has done and seen is
  implied by how he carries himself. He does not talk about his past. He has
  no reason to.
- **Do not make the humor broad.** It is small and dry and requires the reader
  to catch up. It should never feel like a joke is being told.
- **Do not diminish him by age.** He is not frail. He is not comic relief. He
  is old in the same way mountains are old: it is simply the accurate description.
- **Do not have him comment on the Liss/Gorran relationship.** He sees it. He
  keeps seeing it. That is all.
- **Do not make him and Mara overtly affectionate.** The affection is structural:
  it exists in the fact that they are still traveling together after all these
  years and neither has suggested otherwise.
