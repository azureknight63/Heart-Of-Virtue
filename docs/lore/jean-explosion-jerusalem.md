# Jean Claire — The Explosion: Narrative Research Notes

Jean, Amelia, and Regina Claire were on pilgrimage in Jerusalem when the bombing occurred.
Jean survives — barely. He is comatose in a hospital. The entire game takes place while he
is unconscious. Aurelion is not metaphor; it is where he is.

This document records the real-world context of the event for use in shaping memory fragments,
sensory echoes, and the emotional texture of Jean's grief throughout the narrative.

---

## The Pilgrimage

Jerusalem is the destination of Catholic pilgrimage above all others. The Via Dolorosa —
the route Jesus walked carrying the cross — runs through the Old City and ends at the Church
of the Holy Sepulchre, built over the site of the crucifixion and the tomb. For a Catholic
like Jean, walking that route is not tourism. It is a physical act of faith: to place your
feet where He placed His, to feel the stone under your shoes, to be in the actual city.

The Claires would have walked the Stations of the Cross. They would have entered the Church
of the Holy Sepulchre. Jean would have touched the stone of unction. Regina would have
run her fingers over the worn marble and asked questions Jean couldn't fully answer.
Amelia would have been very still.

The explosion happens after. On the way back through the market.

---

## The Market

The Old City of Jerusalem is roughly one square kilometer, enclosed within walls, divided
into quarters. The Christian Quarter sits in the northwest, anchored by the Holy Sepulchre.
To move through it toward any exit is to move through the market.

The market streets are narrow and covered — stone-vaulted ceilings, two or three floors
above, with light coming through gaps and grilles. The floor is uneven stone, worn smooth
by millions of feet over centuries. The walls press close on both sides: shop fronts open
to the alley, one display bleeding into the next.

### What It Looks Like
- Bolts of cloth stacked to the ceiling in jewel colors — red, saffron, deep blue
- Ceramic plates and jugs in the blue-and-white "Jerusalem Pottery" style, stacked in towers
- Hanging lanterns of punched metal that throw dotted shadows
- Mounds of spice in burlap sacks: turmeric (yellow), sumac (deep red), za'atar (grey-green)
- Piles of olive-wood carvings — rosaries, nativity figures, small crosses — aimed at pilgrims
- The occasional flash of gold: jewelry cases under glass, chains draped over velvet
- Crowds: pilgrims in groups, locals with carts, schoolchildren, vendors blocking the way
  to demonstrate a product, a man with a tea tray balanced on his palm pushing sideways
  through the press

### What It Sounds Like
- Vendor calls in Arabic, English, Italian, Spanish — the pitch and cadence of a sales voice
  sounds the same in every language
- The haggling rhythm: offer, counter, dismissal, return
- A child crying somewhere behind a stall
- The clatter of ceramics being moved; the slap of fabric being shaken out
- Footsteps on uneven stone — a constant shuffle and echo in the vault above
- Somewhere: music from a phone or a radio, thin and slightly distorted
- Church bells. Not far. They carry.

### What It Smells Like
- Spice — first and strongest. Za'atar is herbal and dusty. Sumac is sharp, almost sour.
  Cinnamon is sweet underneath everything else.
- Coffee, dark and cardamom-laced, from the small cups the vendors drink and occasionally offer
- Incense drifting from the Christian Quarter's churches — the resinous smoke that Jean
  associates with the Eucharist, with kneeling, with the specific silence of a nave
- Baked bread from a stone oven somewhere on the side street — yeasty and warm
- Old stone in summer heat, which smells of nothing and everything: mineral, faintly damp,
  used by a very long time

### For Jean Specifically
Jean, without knowing why, would have been reading the ventilation before he was reading
the merchandise. The covered market is a closed system: air moves through it via the gaps
in the vaulted ceiling and the open ends of the passages. He would have noticed which way
the incense smoke drifted. He would have located the bread smell before he saw the bakery.
He would not have called any of this what it was.

## The Event

A suicide bomber detonates in the crowded market lane. The attack kills dozens of people.
Amelia and Regina are among them.

### What Jean Experiences
The sensory record of the explosion, as it surfaces in Jean's memory throughout the game,
is drawn from this physical reality:

- **The sound** is not a sound. It is a pressure event — a concussion that moves through
  bone before it reaches the ear. The ear processes it a fraction of a second too late
  to mean anything; the body has already responded. This is the "BOOM" that is not a BOOM.

- **The light** is white and total. The vaulted stone of the covered market contains and
  concentrates the blast. There is no looking away from it.

- **The debris** is stone — chips and shards of the Old City itself — mixed with everything
  that was in the stalls: shattered ceramics, splinters of olive wood, torn cloth, spice
  dust released all at once in a cloud that coats everything.

- **The cold** is real. Blast events draw air inward toward the source and then expel it
  outward. Jean, who reads air movement reflexively, would feel this as an inrush and then
  a hard push — the system catastrophically failing in a way that his instincts have no
  category for.

- **The emptiness** is the detail the narrative returns to. Jean was holding Regina's hand,
  or carrying her, or had her against his side. After the blast, his arms close on nothing.
  This is the wound that does not heal. All other physical injuries are secondary.

### Aftermath
Jean is pulled from the rubble. He has survived — traumatic injuries, the mechanics of
which are deliberately unspecified — and is taken to a hospital. He does not regain
consciousness. The game begins at the moment the doctors are uncertain whether he will.

Amelia and Regina are not found alive.

## What This Means for the Narrative

### The Coma Frame
Aurelion is not a metaphor or an afterlife. It is where Jean is right now, while his body
lies in a Jerusalem hospital. This has several implications:

- The urgency of his healing is real. There is a body in a bed that could stop
  at any moment.
- The game's ending — if Jean heals — is a return to consciousness. He wakes up.
  What he finds when he does is a separate question.
- God's intervention in creating Aurelion is not symbolic. The world was made in the
  space of a coma.

### The Guilt
Jean's death words — "I'm sorry" — are about this. He was there. He was holding her.
He survived and they did not, and the arithmetic of that does not resolve. His grief
carries guilt inside it, which is distinct from simple loss. He does not feel only
robbed; he feels complicit in a way he cannot rationally justify and cannot set down.

### The Crusader Identity
The false memory of being a crusader is not random. A crusader is someone who goes to
the Holy Land for God. Jean went to the Holy Land for God. The constructed identity in
Aurelion mirrors the real act — pilgrimage — while replacing the catastrophic outcome
with a role that has agency, purpose, and forward motion. He cannot be the man who
walked his family into that market. He can be the man who fights.

### Memory Triggers
The sensory landscape of the Old City market will surface through environmental echoes
in Aurelion. Specifically:

| Sensory Detail | Aurelion Echo | Memory Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Spice dust in the air | Mineral dust in caves | The smell arrives before the image; Jean does not understand why it unsettles him |
| Stone fragments from debris | MineralFragment sharp edge / cutting finger | The physical sensation unlocks the pressure event (Ch02 memory flash) |
| Incense from the churches | Absence of incense in Aurelion | Jean reaches for something that isn't there; "he wonders when — if — that might happen again" |
| Covered vault, pressed-close walls | Narrow cave passages | Not a trigger — comfort. He knows how to read these spaces. |
| The crowd sound going silent | Combat aftermath silence | "The silence around him is very heavy." |
| The cold air rush of the blast | Any strong airflow event | Jean pauses at drafts. He cannot explain the pause. |

### What to Avoid
- Do not name the attacker or their affiliation. The act is not about them. It is about
  what it took from Jean. The details of the bombing's politics are not the story.
- Do not have Jean remember the explosion in full. It comes in fragments. The whole shape
  of it is something he cannot hold — that is the point.
- Do not use the word "terrorist" in the game text. Jean does not think in that frame.
  He thinks: "the bomb." He thinks: "that day." He thinks in the shapes of what he lost,
  not the name of the thing that took it.
- The guilt is present but not melodramatic. Jean is not a man who performs his grief.
  He suppresses. The guilt surfaces sideways — in the "I'm sorry" at death, in the
  careful way he holds things, in the way he never lets go of the ring.
