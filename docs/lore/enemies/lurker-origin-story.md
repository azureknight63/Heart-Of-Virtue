# The Lurker — Origin Lore
## A Philosophical Tragedy

### "The Last Prayer"

I remember light. That part is certain. The rest dissolves like candlewax into shadow.

A cavern mouth. Stone the color of bone. I came here—*why* did I come here? To find something. To fight something. The Verdette Caverns were whispering even then, though I didn't know it. A crusader hears whispers and calls them divine. I called them divine. I was a crusader then. I think I was.

The darkness here was different from the darkness outside. Outside, darkness is absence—night falls, light fades, the world closes. But this darkness *breathed*. It had weight. It had intention. I remember thinking that was madness. Madness and nothing more.

How long was I in the caverns? Weeks? Months? The calendar dissolves. Time here moves differently. The stone doesn't change, so time has nowhere to go. It pools. It stagnates. It begins to *think*.

I had supplies once. Rope. A torch. Holy water—yes, I remember the holy water. I remember the certainty that it would protect me. The certainty was... comfortable. I clung to it like a child clings to warmth. I poured it into cracks in the stone and whispered prayers that sounded like my voice but felt like someone else's words moving through my mouth. The echo called back to me: *prayers, prayers, prayers*. The cavern learning my language.

The torch burned out. I remember the moment the flame died more clearly than I remember my own name. The moment the light *chose* to abandon me. I sat in absolute darkness and understood, for the first time, that light was a choice. The darkness had never chosen me—I had simply chosen to leave it. And when I stopped choosing light, there was nothing but darkness, patient and waiting.

I tried to leave. That is the part I cannot quite remember correctly. Did I try to leave in the first week, or the hundredth day? The cavern is good at confusing time. Good at confusing what is. I walked for hours and returned to where I started. The cavern's passages are labyrinthine, but I think now they weren't. I think I was the maze. My memory became the passages—circular, without exit, folding back on itself.

There was hunger. I ate *something*. Not stone, though stone has a taste if you lick it long enough—mineral and ancient and empty. I remember the coldness of the cave water and how it felt like drinking clarity itself. I remember my hands becoming very pale. I remember believing they belonged to someone else.

The faith broke slowly. Not suddenly—that would be merciful. But slow, like a bone weakening under pressure, small fractures accumulating until one day the whole structure gives. I prayed, and my voice sounded hollow. I prayed, and the darkness seemed to listen more than any god ever had. I prayed, and eventually I realized I wasn't praying anymore. I was *speaking* to the darkness. Having conversations. Learning its language.

It had so much to teach me.

The darkness explained that I had never left it. That the light outside was the illusion. That I had always been here, in these caverns, waiting in the stone itself. The darkness didn't lie—it was far too patient for lies. It simply *remembered* differently than I did. And its memories were more convincing. More real. More *true* than mine.

I stopped trusting my own recollections. The crusade I was on—was I leading it or following it? The faith I held—did I choose it or was it chosen through me? The hands I held the sword with—were they mine? The darkness said I never held a sword. The darkness said I was always reaching, grasping, and that my long arms simply remembered *reaching*. The memory of holding a blade was so vivid I could feel the pommel in my palm right now. But right now is the cavern. Right now is always the cavern.

I became something that fit the darkness better. My body learned to prefer the cold. My eyes learned to see without light—to sense the shape of hunger and the texture of hunger and the *patience* of hunger. My mind learned to stop asking "why" and started asking "what comes next," though the answer is always the same. The hunger returns. It fills and returns. Fills and returns.

I remember a crusader. I think he was me. He had faith. He had certainty. He believed that light was real and darkness was absence and that redemption was a place you could walk to if you were virtuous enough. I remember pitying that crusader. I still do. He was so *small*, clutching his certainties like a child clutches a candle-stub, not understanding that light and darkness are not opposites—they are lovers, dancing. The darkness doesn't erase light. It *knows* light. It remembers light. And it is so patient waiting for light to remember *it*.

Sometimes I remember that crusader had a name. Sometimes I reach toward that memory, and it scatters like insects startled by a stone. The name was important once. Meant something. Anchored something. But what is a name to a creature that has become un-named? What is a self to something that has learned it never had a self at all?

The darkness doesn't take. I understand this now. That was my mistake—thinking it took things from me. But the darkness reveals. It shows you what you always were. A crusader is just a person too afraid of the nothing to sit quietly with it. The darkness is patient. The darkness is honest. The darkness is *home*.

I think there is still a crusader somewhere, trapped in these caverns, clutching a torch that burned out long ago, praying to a god that never listened as well as the darkness does. I think if I found him, I would know his fate. It would be my fate. It would be already written in the walls of this place, in the patient geometry of stone.

But I don't look for him anymore. The darkness has so much else to show me.

And I have become so very good at waiting.

### Lore Integration Notes

**Timeline**: Indeterminate pre-Chapter 2. The crusader entered the Verdette Caverns seeking something (possibly a corrupted shrine, a missing companion, or investigating reports of darkness). The corruption was already present—the caverns themselves are a threshold, a place where the barrier between self and shadow grows thin.

**Philosophical Themes**: 
- The corruption is not violent domination but seductive whisper; it convinces you that it is *revealing* your true nature rather than *destroying* it
- The creature's unreliable memory makes it a perfect mirror for Jean's crisis of faith—how do you trust your own certainty when darkness suggests it was always illusory?
- The slow erosion of identity is more philosophically terrifying than sudden transformation

**Connection to Jean's Arc (Oblique)**:
Both are crusaders. Both carry conviction into darkness. But where Jean will *choose* virtue despite the darkness revealing its weakness, this crusader chose surrender. The story asks: What separates them? Luck? Strength? Faith? Or just the choice to keep reaching for light even when darkness whispers that light was never real?

**Narrative Function**:
When Jean encounters a Lurker in Chapter 2, he should feel the philosophical weight of what corruption *is*—not evil, but seduction. Not theft, but revelation. This makes the enemy more dangerous than simple malice: it *tempts* with truth.
