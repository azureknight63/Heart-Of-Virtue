# Heart of Virtue — 15-Minute Spoken Script

*Tone: unhurried, specific, honest. You're not selling — you're describing something you care about to someone who might care too.*

*Pacing note: 15 minutes at a comfortable speaking pace is roughly 1,800–2,000 words. Pause where marked. Let the story breathe.*

---

Let me tell you about a man named Jean Claire.

He's 32. HVAC technician. The kind of person you'd sit next to on a plane and have a perfectly pleasant conversation with, and then not think about again. He takes his faith seriously. Goes to Mass every week, not because it's expected of him but because it's the thing his life is organized around. He saved for years to take his family on a Catholic pilgrimage to Jerusalem — his wife Amelia, his daughter Regina. And they finally went.

It was everything he'd hoped. The city, the history, the feeling of having made it there.

And then, in the Old City market, the day before they were supposed to fly home, there was a bombing.

Jean survived. Amelia and Regina didn't.

He's been in a coma since.

*[Pause.]*

And while he lies in that hospital bed — unconscious, breathing — his mind built him a world. Because the truth was too much to carry awake.

---

He wakes in a place called Aurelion. It's medieval in texture — stone, mist, ancient things. He wakes wearing armor, with a mission he believes in, and a sense of purpose he cannot quite name. He thinks he's a crusader. The identity is borrowed — pieces of a DOS game he played as a kid, *Crusader: Champions of the Holy Lance*, layered over the actual pilgrimage he took, assembled by his subconscious into something that makes it possible to function.

He doesn't remember Amelia. He doesn't remember Regina.

A stone-being named Gorran pulls him from the cave he woke in. Gorran is roughly 1,500 years old. He's enormous, gentle, and nearly nonverbal — he communicates through gestures and single words at first. He's been standing watch over a collapsed passage for centuries, alone. He doesn't explain why he helps Jean. He just does.

They travel together to Grondia — an underground Golemite citadel, austere and ancient. Jean is given shelter in exchange for service: the mineral pools that supply the citadel have been corrupted by slimes, and their king has taken residence at the bottom. Jean clears the pools. He earns his place.

And all the while, in the margins, the memories start coming through.

Fragmentary, out of order. A woman's voice. A ring on a table. A child pressing hard on a crayon. They don't announce themselves. They just arrive, like they do for people who have lost something and haven't finished processing it.

That's Heart of Virtue.

---

Let me talk about what kind of game this is before I go further, because the category matters.

It's a text-based RPG. Browser-playable — you don't download anything, you go to the URL and start. The visual aesthetic is terminal: lime green on black, monospaced fonts, retro. That's a deliberate choice, not a limitation. Text invites imagination in a way that rendered graphics don't. You build Aurelion in your head, and the version you build is yours.

The combat is turn-based and genuinely tactical. Seventy-plus moves across twelve weapon categories — sword, dagger, scythe, spear, mace, polearm, bow, crossbow, unarmed, and more. Stamina-managed, so every action has a cost. Positioning matters: a dagger is useless at range, a bow is useless in a grapple. NPCs have distinct tactical profiles — slimes mob and pressure, Rock Rumblers use their armor to block passages, Cave Bats hit and run. You can have allies fight alongside you, which adds coordination as a layer. Status effects land differently on different enemies.

This is not "click attack repeatedly." It's the kind of combat system that rewards thinking.

But if you walked away from this game having only engaged with the combat, you'd have missed what it is.

---

Jean's arc is structured around the stages of grief. The game doesn't announce this. It embodies it.

Chapter 1 is denial. Jean doesn't know where he is or why he feels the way he feels. He's functional, purposeful, good at surviving. The memories that surface are beautiful and unidentified — he can't place them, can't name them, experiences them as something borrowed from someone else's life.

Chapter 2 is beginning to integrate. The world asks things of Jean and he gives them. He forms bonds. He earns trust. And the memories start carrying weight — he can't explain why a particular stone room, a particular quality of silence, affects him the way it does. His body knows before his mind does.

There's a sequence in the Echoing Caves — a chamber with unusual acoustic properties, where stone surfaces reflect sound back in a particular way — and Jean's reaction to walking through it is visceral without being explained. His companion Gorran says: *"It remembers."* Jean says: *"It's just echo."*

He's right that it's echo. He's wrong about what it remembers.

Chapter 3, currently in development, is the Wailing Badlands. A landscape of broken stone and permanent wind-howl. The locals call it that and stay away. Jean walks into it. Because Chapter 3 is Jean's anger, and you can't skip the anger. You can only walk through it.

*[Pause.]*

The question at the center of this game is: "Why did God let this happen?"

That's the oldest question. It's the question every person who goes through catastrophic loss eventually arrives at, especially people who had strong faith before. And Heart of Virtue does something unusual: it refuses to answer it.

Not because the developer didn't think hard about it — the theological architecture of this game is serious and intentional. But because that's the *honest* answer. The question "why did God let this happen" does not get a satisfying resolution in real life. People who go through it either get destroyed by the unanswered question, or they find a way to carry it. And the game is about the second path.

Jean's specific guilt is worth naming precisely, because it's not "why did God let this happen." It's: *I chose Jerusalem. I planned the route. I put them in that market.* That's different. That's more specific, more personal, and therefore harder. You can't theodicy your way out of "I made the choice that put my family in the path of the bomb." You can only eventually arrive at something more honest than certainty. Which is what Jean, very slowly, does.

---

The companion Gorran is one of the most architecturally interesting characters in the game, and I want to spend a moment on him.

He starts nearly nonverbal. By the end of Chapter 1, he has a handful of words. By Chapter 2, simple phrases. He and Jean are building a shared language together from scratch, and it's a whole arc on its own — Gorran's vocabulary expands as the game progresses, and by the time the story needs him most, he's able to say:

*"Earth takes. I have not... understood why. Was angry. For long time. Then not angry. Do not know if... better."*

That's his most complex utterance. He says it at Jean's worst moment — the faith crisis, the collapse. And it lands because players watched him build toward it across the entire game. He's not offering an answer. He's offering company in the question. Which is the only thing that actually helps.

Gorran also lost someone. His life partner, in a rockslide, centuries before the game begins. He never explains it. He's had so long to carry it that the carrying is just part of how he stands. And Jean, who is very early in his own carrying, is watching what it looks like to have done it for a long time. That's what the friendship is.

---

There's a second companion, a woman named Mara. She's 27, nomadic, sardonic, reads situations the way archaeologists read strata — paying attention to what's beneath, not what's on the surface. She joins Jean's party later in the story.

She's been carrying a crucifix she found in the ruins of some settlement. It's just an artifact to her — pretty, old, probably worth something. She doesn't know where it came from. She doesn't know that it came from a place like the one Amelia would have visited, before everything. She doesn't know that Jean is going to recognize it, eventually.

The moment he does — the moment Jean understands what Mara has been carrying, unknowingly, between them, the whole time — is a late-game revelation that reframes a lot of what came before. It's the kind of storytelling where you go back and read the earlier scenes differently afterward.

Where the game stands right now: Chapters 1 and 2 are complete and free to play in your browser. That's roughly one to two hours of content — the full Chapter 1 arc, the Grondia arc, the mineral pools, the King Slime boss fight, the river crossing, the Echoing Caves sequence. A complete story beat with a genuine cliffhanger.

Chapter 3 — the Wailing Badlands — has its lore fully designed, its map built, and is in active development. Beyond that, the story runs five to seven chapters at full release, with an estimated twenty-five to thirty-five hours of content. The roadmap includes the Resolute Plains, a Sanctuary of Illusions sequence, the portal Jean finds and chooses not to go through, and the quiet ending where faith begins to return off-screen.

The game is built on a modern stack: Flask API, React frontend, cloud saves, an in-game feedback system that routes reports directly to development. Eighty-seven percent test coverage. Built to last.

The goal right now is a hundred players who have actually finished Chapter 1. Not impressions. Not follows. People who sat down with Jean Claire, walked into Grondia, fought a King Slime, and came out the other side knowing who they'd just spent time with.

If that's you — and you'll know within the first thirty minutes whether this is your kind of game — then welcome. And the ask is simple: tell someone. The way you'd share a book. With a sentence about what it is, to someone specific who might need it.

There are people who need a game that takes grief seriously. Who need a story that doesn't flinch from the question that doesn't have an answer. Who want tactical combat and genuine narrative depth in the same package, and aren't finding it in the mainstream.

Heart of Virtue is for them.
