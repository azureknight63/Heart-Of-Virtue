# Heart of Virtue — 5-Minute Leave-Behind

---

## The Setup

Jean Claire is 32. HVAC technician. Weekly Mass, rosary on the nightstand, pilgrimage planned for years. He finally took his family — his wife Amelia, his daughter Regina — to Jerusalem.

They were in the Old City market when the bombing happened.

Jean survived. They didn't.

He's been in a coma since. And while he lies in that hospital bed, his mind is somewhere else entirely.

---

## The World

Inside Jean's coma, there is a world called **Aurelion** — vast, ancient, inhabited by stone-beings called Golemites and scattered human nomads. Jean wakes there believing he is a crusader. He has armor, a mission, a companion named Gorran who pulled him out of a cave.

He does not know Aurelion was made for him.

He does not know his family is dead.

His subconscious sealed the wound behind the only story it knew how to tell — a childhood DOS game he played obsessively, *Crusader: Champions of the Holy Lance*, blended with the pilgrimage he actually took. The crusader identity is a shell. Underneath it, the grief is waiting.

**Heart of Virtue** is the story of what happens when the shell breaks.

---

## The Game

This is a **text-based narrative RPG**, playable in your browser, free to try. It blends two things that rarely coexist at this quality:

**1. Literary-grade story.** Jean's arc moves through the stages of grief without announcing them. His guilt is specific and earned: *I chose Jerusalem. I planned the route. I put them there.* His faith doesn't collapse into atheism — it collapses into silence, which is harder and truer. His companion Gorran, a 1,500-year-old Golemite who lost his own partner centuries ago, mirrors Jean's grief in a non-Christian spiritual framework. Their friendship is built on silence and shared weight.

The game asks "Why did God let this happen?" and then — unlike almost every piece of media that touches this question — refuses to answer it. What it offers instead is Jean learning to carry the question rather than be destroyed by it.

**2. Tactical depth.** Combat is turn-based, stamina-managed, and built around 70+ moves across a dozen weapon types: swords, daggers, maces, bows, scythes, spears. Positioning matters — a dagger is useless at range, a bow is useless in a grapple. NPCs have distinct tactical profiles. You can have allies fight alongside you, requiring coordination. Status effects, resistances, weapon experience that grows as you specialize — this is not "click attack repeatedly."

## The World in Detail

Aurelion is a world with geography, factions, and a creation mythology you piece together as you play.

- **Grondia** — an underground Golemite citadel, austere and ancient, where Jean is given shelter and purpose in Chapters 1–2.
- **Grondelith Mineral Pools** — your first major quest: the pools are corrupted by slimes and their king. Your success earns you standing in Grondia.
- **The Echoing Caves** — a chamber that resonates acoustically with the stone silence of the market where Jean's family died. His body knows before his mind does.
- **The Wailing Badlands** (Chapter 3, in development) — a landscape of broken stone and omnipresent wind-wail that is, structurally, Jean's unprocessed anger made into terrain.

Scattered through Aurelion are objects that shouldn't logically exist: a child's drawing, a crucifix, a prayer carved in stone. Players who are paying attention will understand why. Jean never will.

---

## The Companions

**Gorran** is the companion the game is built around. He begins nearly nonverbal — guttural sounds, gestures — and over the course of the game builds a shared language with Jean from scratch. By Chapter 3, his most complex line is: *"Earth takes. I have not... understood why. Was angry. For long time. Then not angry. Do not know if... better."* He says it at exactly the moment Jean needs to hear it.

**Mara** is a sardonic 27-year-old nomadic artifact trader who joins Jean later. She carries a crucifix she found in some ruins — doesn't know what it means, doesn't know whose it was. Players who figure out whose it was will understand what the game is doing before Jean does.

## Why This Game

Most games about faith are either hagiography or dismissive. Most games about grief are about getting over it. Most narrative RPGs sacrifice mechanical depth for story or story for mechanics.

Heart of Virtue is built by someone who understands both code and theology, and who made a game that takes both seriously.

If you've ever carried a question that didn't have an answer, and kept going anyway — this game is about that.

**Play the free beta. Tell someone it exists.**
