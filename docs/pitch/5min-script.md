# Heart of Virtue — 5-Minute Spoken Script

*Tone: conversational storyteller. You're describing a game you love, not selling something.*

---

I want to tell you about a game called Heart of Virtue.

The main character is a man named Jean Claire. He's 32. HVAC technician. Goes to Mass every week. He's the kind of person who takes faith seriously — not performatively, but as the actual organizing principle of his life. He saved up for years to take his family on a Catholic pilgrimage to Jerusalem. His wife Amelia. His daughter Regina. They finally went.

And in the Old City market, there was a bombing. Jean survived. They didn't.

He's been in a coma since.

And while he lies in that hospital bed — in the real world — his mind is somewhere else. It built him a world. A medieval world called Aurelion, with stone-beings called Golemites, ancient citadels, corrupted mineral pools, creatures that want to kill you. Jean wakes there believing he's a crusader. He has a mission. He has a companion named Gorran — a 1,500-year-old Golemite who found him in a cave.

He doesn't remember Amelia. He doesn't remember Regina. His subconscious sealed that away because it was too much to carry.

That's the starting premise of Heart of Virtue. And the whole game is about what happens as that shell — the crusader identity — starts to crack.

---

So mechanically, what is this? It's a text-based RPG. Browser-playable — you don't download anything, you just go to the URL and start. The aesthetic is terminal-green-on-black, retro, deliberately evocative of the old text adventures. The combat is turn-based and genuinely tactical — 70-plus moves across a dozen weapon types, stamina management, positioning that matters because a dagger is useless at range and a bow is useless in a grapple. NPCs have distinct fighting styles. You can have allies fighting alongside you. It's the kind of combat system where you think.

But the reason to play this game is the story.

Jean's arc is structured around the stages of grief — and unlike most media that invokes that concept, this game doesn't announce it. You just find yourself in the anger stage, or the denial stage, because the world Jean is moving through *is* those stages, made into terrain. Chapter 3 takes place in the Wailing Badlands — a landscape of broken stone and permanent wind-howl that is, literally, Jean's unprocessed rage externalized.

The companion Gorran starts the game nearly nonverbal. He communicates through gestures and single words. Over the course of the game, he and Jean build a shared language together, and it's a whole character arc on its own. By the time the story needs him most, he can say: *"Earth takes. I have not... understood why. Was angry. For long time. Then not angry. Do not know if... better."* And that lands because you watched him build to it.

There's another companion, a woman named Mara, who carries a crucifix she found in the ruins. She doesn't know whose it was. Players who are paying attention will figure out whose it was before Jean does.

---

The central question of the game — the one it's actually asking — is: "Why did God let this happen?"

And here's the honest answer the game gives: it doesn't answer it.

Not because the developer didn't think about it — the theological architecture of this game is serious — but because that's the true answer. Real people who go through real loss don't get that question answered. They either get destroyed by it, or they find a way to carry it. Jean's arc is about the second thing. Not faith restored. Not the theodicy explained. Faith transformed — worn, honest, his own. The kind that only exists in people who've actually been through it.

If you've read C.S. Lewis's "A Grief Observed" — the one he wrote after his wife died, not the polished apologetics — this game is in that register.
