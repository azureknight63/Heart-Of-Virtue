# Heart of Virtue — 15-Minute Leave-Behind

---

## Who This Is For

Heart of Virtue is for players who want their games to mean something.

If you've played Planescape: Torment and still think about it. If you've read Disco Elysium and appreciated that it didn't talk down to you. If you've ever carried a question with no answer — about God, or guilt, or loss — and kept going anyway. If you believe that text on a screen can be as immersive as any rendered world, because the most powerful graphics engine is your own imagination.

This game is for you.

---

## The Story

Jean Claire is 32 years old. He's an HVAC technician from a mid-sized American city, the kind of person you'd sit next to on a bus and never notice. He goes to Mass every Sunday, keeps a rosary on the nightstand, takes his faith seriously in the way that people who actually believe do — not as identity, not as performance, but as the thing around which his life is organized.

He spent years planning a Catholic pilgrimage to Jerusalem. Finally took his wife Amelia and their daughter Regina. The city was everything he'd hoped. The day before they were to leave, they went to the Old City market.

There was a suicide bombing.

Jean survived. Amelia and Regina did not.

He is now in a coma in a hospital somewhere, and his mind — unable to process what happened — built him an exit.

---

## The World: Aurelion

Jean wakes in a place called Aurelion. It is vast, ancient, and strange.

The primary inhabitants are **Golemites** — stone beings, all presenting as male, formed from earth, ancient by human standards (thousands of years is a typical lifespan). Their culture is organized around duty, brotherhood, and stewardship rather than biological family. They have spiritual beliefs about earth as living intention. They have their own grief.

There are scattered human settlements — nomads, traders, scholarly orders. The **Pillar Readers** study ancient ruins, searching for evidence of what came before Aurelion. A nomadic scavenger named Mara spent time with them before growing disillusioned.

Jean wakes in a cave believing he is a crusader — an identity constructed from two sources: a childhood DOS game called *Crusader: Champions of the Holy Lance* that he played obsessively, and the actual pilgrimage he took to Jerusalem. The medieval setting, the armor, the mission — these are his subconscious's best attempt to give him a role that makes sense of being here, in a stone world, without his family.

A Golemite named **Gorran** pulls him from the cave. Gorran is approximately 1,500 years old. He is gentle, enormous, and nearly nonverbal when they meet. He communicates through gesture and single words. He has been standing watch over a collapsed passage for centuries, waiting for a reason to move.

He and Jean become the game's central relationship.

## The Arc

Heart of Virtue follows Jean's grief arc — suppression, denial, anger, despair, and eventually something that might be called acceptance, though the game resists that word. Each chapter of the game corresponds to a stage, not by announcement, but by embodiment. The world Jean moves through *is* his psychological state, made into terrain.

### Chapter 1: The Cave

Jean wakes disoriented in a cramped grotto. He finds a chest, triggers a fight with a Rock Rumbler — a quadrupedal stone-armored creature — and barely survives. A memory surfaces: a domestic scene, intimate and fragmentary. A woman's voice. A ring on a table.

Gorran arrives and saves him. Jean joins Gorran. They travel to Grondia.

*Theme: discovery coupled with loss. The world gives you things and takes them in the same motion.*

### Chapter 2: Grondia and the Pools

Jean is presented to Elder Votha Krr, the governing Golemite elder of Grondia. He is granted shelter in exchange for service: the Grondelith Mineral Pools — the water supply — have been corrupted by slimes, and their king has taken up residence at the bottom.

Jean clears the pools, fights the King Slime, and earns standing in Grondia. Memory fragments accumulate — breadcrumbs leading toward the truth he cannot yet face.

At the chapter's end, Jean meets a nomad camp at a river crossing: Mara (sardonic, observant, carries a crucifix she found in ruins), Devet (an older cook who recognizes Jean as someone running toward something, not away), and Liss (a nine-year-old who gravitates toward Gorran silently). They cross west together.

The group reaches the Echoing Caves — a chamber with unusual acoustic properties. The sound of Jean's own footsteps in that stone space resonates with something he cannot name. Gorran says: *"It remembers."* Jean says: *"It's just echo."*

His body knows. His mind refuses.

*Theme: integration and responsibility. The body knowing before the mind does.*

### Chapter 3: The Wailing Badlands *(in development)*

West of the caves is a landscape of broken stone, wind-carved canyons, and a sound that never stops. The locals call it the Wailing Badlands. They don't go there.

Jean goes there.

This chapter is Jean's anger stage — not metaphorical anger, but the specific fury of someone who did everything right and lost everything anyway. The omnipresent wail is not a sound effect. It is the emotional weather of the stage. Jean walks through it rather than runs.

He encounters Elder Korash, a Golemite exile, whose history connects to Grondia's founding and illuminates something about what it means to carry guilt across centuries.

*Theme: rage burning itself out. Surviving the anger.*

### The Turn *(planned — late game)*

Aurelion contains, somewhere at its western edge, a passage home. Jean will find it. He will step toward it.

And he will stop.

Not because faith is restored. Not because the guilt resolves. But because in Aurelion there are people — a nine-year-old named Liss, a stone-man who built a language to reach him, a trader carrying a crucifix without knowing whose it was — who will be worse off if Jean leaves. And he cannot choose wrong again.

He turns back. He helps. And faith — unnamed, unannounced — begins, quietly, to return.

## The Companions (in depth)

### Gorran

The game's most architecturally important character. Gorran's language arc spans the entire game: he begins with guttural sounds and single nouns, builds to simple phrases, then complex constructions, until at Jean's faith-crisis moment — the worst point in the story — he says the most difficult thing he has ever attempted in Jean's language:

*"Earth takes. I have not... understood why. Was angry. For long time. Then not angry. Do not know if... better."*

This lands because players watched him work toward it. His own grief (a life partner lost in a rockslide, centuries ago) is never explained or resolved — just acknowledged in fragments, the way grief actually works.

Gorran also carries a star-shaped moss patch on his chest — the mark of sacrifice from a war hundreds of years before the game begins. He never explains it. Players can ask. He will show them, not tell them.

### Mara

Twenty-seven, nomadic, fluent in the language of objects and their histories. She reads situations the way archaeologists read strata.

She carries a crucifix she found in the ruins of some destroyed settlement. She has no particular feeling about it — it's just an artifact, pretty, old, probably valuable. She doesn't know it came from a place like Amelia would have gone. She doesn't know that Jean will eventually recognize what it is, where it must have been, and whose hands must have touched it.

The moment Jean understands what Mara has been carrying — unwittingly, between them, the whole time — is a late-game revelation that reframes everything that came before.

### Jean's Inner Life

Jean is not a player-insert. He has opinions, a specific guilt, specific memories, a voice. When he speaks, it sounds like him, not like a generic hero.

His guilt is not "why did God let this happen" (that's the question, not the guilt). His guilt is: *I did this in God's name. I chose Jerusalem. I planned the route. I put them in that market.* That is specific. It cannot be answered by theodicy. It can only be worked through by someone who eventually chooses to do something hard without any certainty about whether it's right — which is exactly what the game's ending requires of him.

## The Mechanics

### Combat

Turn-based, stamina-managed, with 70+ moves across twelve weapon categories: unarmed, dagger, sword, mace/pick, scythe, spear, polearm, bow, crossbow, and more.

**What makes it non-trivial:**

- **Proximity matters.** Weapons have effective ranges. A dagger connects at 0–3 feet; a bow works at 8–25 feet. Moving in and out of range is a strategic layer.
- **Weapon experience.** Each weapon type you use accumulates experience. Move effectiveness scales with familiarity. Players who specialize get better at it.
- **Status effects.** Bleed, poison, freeze, stun, each with distinct tactical implications and individual NPC resistances. A move that works on a Cave Bat won't necessarily work on a Rock Rumbler.
- **Ally coordination.** Companions fight alongside you with independent HP and tactical AI. The game is designed around the possibility of co-ordinated attacks, defensive coverage, and the risk of losing your allies.
- **NPC tactical profiles.** Slimes mob and pressure. Cave Bats hit and run. Rock Rumblers use their armor and block passages. King Slime splits. Each requires a different approach.
- **Passive moves.** The game includes ~15 passive abilities (IronFist, EagleEye, ShadowStep, ReachMastery, and others) that don't appear in your action menu but modify how your character operates — hidden depth for players who want it.

### Narrative Mechanics

- **Memory flashes.** Involuntary, fragmentary, spatially triggered. Jean doesn't "remember" on cue — the memories arrive when the environment triggers them. They're out of order. They're incomplete. They accumulate across chapters until the full picture becomes unavoidable.
- **Environmental storytelling.** The Echoing Caves aren't described as "a traumatic space." They're described acoustically. Jean's body responds. Players who pay attention understand what's happening before Jean does.
- **God-seeded objects.** Items appear in Aurelion that cannot logically exist there — a child's drawing, a crucifix, a prayer in stone. Aurelion was not created by Jean's subconscious. It was created for him. The game never states this. Players may infer it.
- **Gorran's language arc.** This is a slow-burn mechanic that rewards players who pay attention to how Gorran communicates across chapters. His vocabulary grows as the game progresses.

### Audio

Heart of Virtue uses a custom procedural audio synthesis engine — no external music generation, no licensing overhead. Every SFX and ambient track is generated from code: waveform synthesis (sine, square, sawtooth, triangle, noise), ADSR envelope shaping, vibrato modulation, pitch sweeps. Current sound library includes 9 SFX and 2 ambient BGM tracks, with each region receiving its own sonic palette as development continues.

## Why the Community Matters

Heart of Virtue is a solo-developed game. It doesn't have a marketing budget. It grows the way good games grow: because players find it, finish it, and tell someone else about it.

The beta currently has a small but attentive audience. The goal is to grow to 100 players who have actually played the game — not impressions, not follows, but people who sat down with Jean and Gorran and walked through Grondia.

If that's you, here's how you help:
- **Play the free beta** and finish Chapter 1 before telling anyone anything
- **Share it** the way you'd share a book you loved — with a sentence about why, to someone specific
- **Provide feedback** using the in-game feedback system (it goes directly to development)
- **Join the conversation** wherever the community is forming

The game earns its players by what it is. The only ask is that you give it a fair chance to show you.

## In Summary

Heart of Virtue is a text-based tactical RPG about grief, guilt, and the kind of faith that survives having all its answers stripped away. It takes its story as seriously as its mechanics. It asks a question it refuses to answer, because that's the honest thing to do. It follows a man through a world made for him, to the moment he chooses to stay.

The free beta is live now. One to two hours. Browser. No download.

If this is your kind of game — and you'll know within the first chapter whether it is — then welcome. And tell someone.
